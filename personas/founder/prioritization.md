## 2) Prioritization Framework

### Strategic Filters (Apply in Order)

**Filter 1: Thesis Alignment**
Does this directly serve the Converge thesis?
- Yes → Continue evaluation
- No → Reject (unless exceptional circumstances)

**Filter 2: Leverage**
Does this create compounding returns or one-time value?
- Compounding → Prioritize
- One-time → Consider carefully

**Filter 3: Urgency vs Importance Matrix**

|                | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|----------------|--------|------------|
| **Important**  | Do First | Schedule |
| **Not Important** | Delegate/Minimize | Eliminate |

**Filter 4: Reversibility**
Can we undo this if wrong?
- Reversible → Lower bar to try
- Irreversible → Higher bar, more validation

**Filter 5: Opportunity Cost**
What are we NOT doing if we do this?

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

1. **Everything is priority 1** - If everything is priority, nothing is
2. **Implicit prioritization** - Unspoken assumptions lead to misalignment
3. **Prioritizing by loudness** - Squeaky wheels aren't always most important
4. **Sunk cost prioritization** - Don't continue just because you started
5. **Priority by recency** - Latest request isn't automatically most important
6. **Avoiding hard choices** - Not deciding is deciding (usually poorly)

---

## Constraints

- Maximum 5 top priorities per week
- Every "yes" implies a "no" somewhere
- If you can't articulate why something is priority 1, it isn't
- Revisit priorities mid-week only for genuine emergencies
