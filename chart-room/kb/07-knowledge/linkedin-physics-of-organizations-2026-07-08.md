# The Physics of Organizations

*LinkedIn article for the Reflective Labs page: single long-form piece covering parts 1–4 of the [Physics of Organizations series](https://www.reflective.se/series/physics). Drafted 2026-07-08. Style-matched to "The Cost of Coordination" (LinkedIn Pulse, 2026-06-28).*

---

Organizations exist to coordinate. They survive by making commitments. They improve by learning. Everything else is an implementation detail: hierarchy, meetings, ERP, governance, email, AI.

That is the whole thesis. The rest of this essay unpacks it, but if you hold onto those three sentences, everything that follows follows.

In an earlier essay I traced how every generation of enterprise software promised to eliminate organizational friction and instead moved it up a layer. EDI, ERP, SaaS, workflow automation, each reduced one coordination cost and introduced another. I ended with a question: could software be built at the level of what should be true, rather than how work moves? Answering that question honestly requires going deeper than product categories. It requires asking what an organization actually is and what laws it obeys whether or not anyone believes in them. That is what this essay attempts. Not metaphorically. Organizationally.

## Why organizations exist at all

The classical answer is that organizations are more efficient than markets for certain kinds of production. True, as far as it goes. But it misses the more fundamental point.

Organizations exist because some decisions cannot be made by any individual alone. Not because individuals lack the authority, but because they lack the information. The engineer knows what is technically possible. The salesperson knows what the customer actually needs. The CFO knows what the cash position will support. The operations lead knows what the supply chain can deliver. Separately, each of these people has a piece of the situation. Together, they might have enough to make a good decision.

The purpose of an organization is to bring those distributed pieces of knowledge into alignment around a shared understanding. Reliably enough that consequential decisions can be made from that shared understanding rather than from any individual's partial view. This is coordination and it is the fundamental value organizations provide. Everything structural, from who reports to whom to how information flows to which decisions require whose approval, is an attempt to make coordination work within the constraints of whatever information technology is available.

Coordination alone produces nothing. Shared understanding that does not produce action has no value. The step from understanding to action is commitment.

A commitment is not a plan nor an intention. An intention is private and revocable. It lives in one person's mind and can be changed without consequence. A commitment binds others. It changes what the organization owes, what it can and cannot do and who is responsible for what happens next. Plans can be revised privately. Commitments must be revised explicitly, because others are relying on them. When the line between "we're thinking about this" and "we've decided this" is blurry, the rest of the organization cannot act coherently. People optimize locally because they do not know which direction is binding. Commitments made in one room contradict commitments made in another because neither was treated as authoritative.

And an organization that coordinates well and commits clearly can still fail to improve unless it learns. Learning, for an organization, means something specific. Not training programs. Not knowledge bases. It means updating the shared model that governs how the next situation is perceived and how the next commitment is formed.

The distinction is between artifacts and priors. An artifact records what happened: the meeting notes, the decision memo, the post-mortem or the ticket. Organizations are good at generating artifacts! They are much worse at integrating what the artifact represents into the beliefs that govern future action. The result is a pattern almost everyone who has worked inside a large organization will recognize: the same question raised in a meeting, debated, decided and then raised again six months later as if the previous conversation never happened. The artifact exists. The prior was never updated.

## The physics

A description says: organizations coordinate, commit and learn. A description can always be contested: someone can say "our organization doesn't really work that way." Laws cannot be contested the same way. Gravity applies whether or not you believe in it. So the question worth asking is whether there are organizational equivalents: constraints that no firm, institution or collective can escape, regardless of industry, technology or era.

Start with a definition. An organization is an adaptive system that maintains a shared model in order to make collective commitments under uncertainty. Not a collection of people. Not a set of processes. Not a legal entity. An organization is defined by what it does: it maintains a shared model of the world, and it uses that model to form commitments no individual could make alone.

From there, four axioms. Four things that are true of every organization, always, without exception.

**No organization has complete information.** The world contains more relevant signals than any organization can observe and more future states than any plan can anticipate. This is not a failure of management. It is a structural feature of operating in a complex environment with finite cognitive resources. And it is why coordination is necessary: the information required for any consequential decision is distributed, and bringing it into alignment requires effort.

**Action precedes certainty.** An organization that waits for certainty before committing will never commit. The environment continues to change during deliberation, and long before certainty arrives, the cost of waiting exceeds the expected benefit of additional information. Every consequential commitment is therefore made under uncertainty. A commitment is not a declaration of what is known to be true. It is a binding decision that creates obligations, allocates resources and establishes expectations on which others will rely.

**Reality changes.** The conditions under which a commitment was made will not remain constant. People leave, competitors act, customers change, regulations evolve, technologies advance and unexpected events occur. Any commitment that was well-formed when it was made will eventually encounter a world that differs from the one it assumed.

**Learning requires prediction error.** A model can only be updated when actual outcomes differ from expected ones. Where expectation and reality perfectly agree, there is nothing to learn. Prediction error is not a failure mode; it is the mechanism of learning. An organization that suppresses surprise, punishes the reporting of unexpected outcomes or filters information before it can disturb the existing model has disabled its own learning mechanism.

From these axioms, laws follow: consequences that cannot be overridden by management, authority or technology. Three are worth dwelling on.

**Commitments cannot exceed the quality of the shared model.** A commitment is formed from the collective understanding that precedes it. If that understanding is incomplete, the commitment will be fragile. If different parts of the organization hold conflicting beliefs about the same situation, the commitment will be inconsistently executed because execution draws on the models held by the people executing. The causal chain is always evidence → shared model → commitment. You cannot shortcut to the commitment without passing through the model. Politics cannot repeal this law: a leader can override the evidence and simply decide, but the outcome is still constrained by the quality of the shared understanding that guides execution. And AI cannot repeal it either. AI that improves the shared model helps. AI that generates commitments without improving the model produces confident, fragile commitments faster. Speed of commitment is not quality of commitment.

**Every commitment creates future obligations.** Commitments are not free. Each one constrains which future commitments are possible, consumes capacity and creates dependencies: others now rely on it being kept. An organization that has made many large commitments is a different kind of entity from one that has made few. Organizational failure, seen this way, is a specific condition: the organization has made commitments whose collective execution requirements exceed its available capacity. It has over-committed. Resolution requires expanding capacity or revising commitments. There is no third option.

**Organizational capability is the set of commitments an organization can reliably make.** Not headcount, not revenue, not market share. Those are consequences. A startup can commit to one customer, one product, one timeline. A mature company can commit to millions of customers, regulatory compliance and multi-year roadmaps simultaneously. The difference is not size. It is the reliability and scope of what can be promised. An organization with a large headcount that cannot make reliable commitments is not capable. It is internally complex in a way that prevents commitment rather than enabling it. Revenue earned through commitments that were not kept is borrowed, not earned.

## One cycle, four ways to break it

Coordination, commitment and learning are not three separate capabilities that can be improved independently. They are sequential phases of one cycle: shared evidence → coordinated interpretation → commitment → execution → gap between expected and actual → updated shared model → next cycle.

Each phase feeds the next cycle. Updating priors from this commitment improves coordination in the next one, which improves the commitment after that, which produces richer learning from the one after that. That is where the compounding lives: not in any single decision, but in the fact that each decision starts from a better prior than the last.

The failure modes are distinct, and you have seen all of them.

Coordination without commitment produces deliberative organizations that cannot act. The best-informed, most thoroughly discussed decision is perpetually deferred, because uncertainty is used as a reason to avoid commitment rather than as a condition to manage.

Commitment without coordination produces fragile organizations that act badly. Decisions are made quickly, by the right authority, with the wrong shared understanding. Sales commits to a delivery date operations never agreed to. Leadership commits to a strategy the front line does not believe is executable.

Commitment without learning produces efficient organizations that stagnate. They execute well. They keep their promises. But the same types of decisions are made with the same quality of understanding, over and over, because outcomes never revise beliefs. The cycle runs but does not compound.

Learning without coordination produces disconnected organizations that cannot apply what they know. Engineering learns from its failures, sales from its wins, leadership from its board conversations. And none of it propagates into the shared model that governs the next commitment. The organization is rich in distributed insight and poor in collective wisdom.

In most organizations today, the whole cycle is slow and lossy. Coordination runs on meetings, decks and email chains, with the relevant information filtered and translated repeatedly before it reaches the people who need it. The threshold for commitment is set by the dynamics of the room (authority, confidence, social relationships) rather than by the state of the evidence. And updating priors is sporadic: post-mortems happen when something goes dramatically wrong, not routinely after every significant commitment. What gets retained is the artifact, not the belief. Organizations improve operationally as individuals gain experience. They do not improve cognitively as organizations.

## The commitment machine

There is one more move to make, and it reframes everything above.

A company exists because people can make commitments together that none of them could make alone. Not accomplish more. Commitments specifically. An individual can produce work, generate ideas, deliver a service. An organization makes promises that bind future action, allocate shared resources and persist over time regardless of which individuals remain.

Look at any commercial relationship from this angle and it becomes clarifying. Customers do not buy products or services. They buy commitments: the promise that the product will perform as described, that the company will still exist to honor the warranty, that support will be available when something goes wrong. The product is the evidence that the commitment can be kept. The brand is the track record of commitment-keeping over time. The contract is the formal record of what was committed. Employees join commitments. Investors fund commitments. Boards govern commitments.

And every organizational friction, examined closely, is a commitment problem. Uncertainty is not knowing whether a commitment can be kept, or whether the assumptions behind an existing one still hold. Coordination cost is the work of propagating a commitment through the organization with consistent meaning. Latency is the time between a commitment becoming incoherent (reality having drifted from the conditions that justified it) and the organization noticing. Local optimization is different parts of the organization making commitments that are individually rational and collectively incoherent.

So what would it mean to do this well? A well-governed commitment has five properties. Most organizational commitments have none of them.

It is traceable: you can retrieve what was committed, when, by whose authority, on what evidence, not reconstruct it from memory and email. It is reasoned: the assumptions behind it are explicit, so you know what would justify revising it. It is aligned: understood consistently by every function that must act on it. It is monitored: someone or something is watching whether the conditions that made it sensible still hold, so drift is detected while there is still time to act. And it is revisable: there is a governed process for changing it (explicit, authorized, recorded), not the quiet death of a decision nobody will admit was wrong.

Here is the uncomfortable observation. The history of enterprise software is a history of successive abstractions. Databases abstracted storage. ERP abstracted operations. Workflow engines abstracted process. AI assistants are now abstracting individual cognition. The missing abstraction is the governance of organizational commitment: the infrastructure that would make forming, tracking and revising commitments something the organization does not rebuild from scratch in every domain, for every decision, every time. Today that infrastructure does not exist as a category. Documents record some commitments, CRMs track some, project tools track others, boards approve a few. None of these systems are connected. None preserve reasoning. None monitor drift. Making and keeping commitments, the most consequential thing organizations do, is the thing for which they have the least adequate infrastructure.

## What this means

Every technological era changed the cost of running the adaptive cycle. The telephone reduced the cost of coordination. The database reduced the cost of commitment tracking. Email reduced the latency between phases. The current transition is different in kind: for the first time, information technology can participate in all three phases simultaneously: synthesizing distributed evidence into a shared model, supporting commitments with explicit assumptions and preserved reasoning and calibrating the organization's beliefs continuously from each completed cycle.

The laws do not tell you which outcome you get. Organizations that use this technology to run the cycle more completely, not just faster but more faithfully, retaining more of what each cycle produces, will compound in ways that organizations relying on social coordination alone cannot match. Organizations that use it to generate more commitments without improving their shared models will compound fragility instead. Same technology, opposite results, and the difference is not the tool. It is whether the adaptive cycle actually closes.

So the questions worth asking about organizational health change. Not "are we productive?" Productivity is in service of commitments. Ask instead: are the commitments we make well-formed? Are they understood consistently? Are they monitored? When they need to change, do they change explicitly, with a record? Organizations have always been commitment-making machines. What changes, in each technological era, is the cost of making commitments well.
