# LinkedIn Post — The Physics of Organizations

*Companion post announcing the [[linkedin-physics-of-organizations-2026-07-08]] article. Drafted 2026-07-08. Style: no em-dashes, no serial commas. Two versions: company page (measured) and personal profile (bolder).*

---

Every generation of enterprise software promised to remove organizational friction. EDI, ERP, SaaS, workflow automation. Each one removed a cost and moved the friction up a layer.

We are about to do it again. The instinct right now is to patch: bolt AI onto the ERP, add a copilot to the CRM, automate one more workflow. Patching assumes the systems are right and just need to be faster.

They are not right. They were built around how work moves, not around what an organization actually is.

Strip everything away and three functions remain. Organizations coordinate, they commit and they learn. One loop: shared evidence, a shared model, a commitment, execution, a gap between expected and actual, an updated model. Every friction you have ever felt at work is that loop running slowly, or not closing at all.

So invert it. Don't fit the organization into applications. Start from the physics and build around the loop. Then make the loop better with every cycle: commitments that are traceable, reasoned, aligned, monitored and revisable. Each decision starting from a better prior than the last.

That is not a faster system of record. It is collective intelligence, and it is what we are building at Reflective Labs.

The full argument is in the essay: why organizations exist, the four axioms, the laws that follow and why the most consequential thing organizations do is the thing with the least adequate infrastructure.

## Personal profile version (bolder)

Every business system you run today was designed for a world where humans had to be the middleware. That world just ended.

This is why no enterprise software designed before 2025 can be right. Not wrong in its code. Wrong in its premise. EDI, ERP, SaaS, workflow automation, all of it was built around how work moves, and all of it left the real integration to people: carrying context between tools, re-keying the same data, translating decisions from one department's language into another's, chasing status in meetings. We call it work. Most of it is being the glue between systems that were never built to think together.

The instinct now is to patch: bolt AI onto the ERP, add a copilot to the CRM, automate one more workflow. That makes the middleware job faster. It does not end the job.

Strip everything away and an organization does three things. It coordinates, it commits and it learns. One loop: shared evidence, a shared model, a commitment, execution, a gap between expected and actual, an updated model. Every friction you have ever felt at work is that loop running slowly, or not closing at all.

So invert it. Don't fit the organization into applications. Start from the physics of organizations and build around the loop. Make the loop better with every cycle: commitments that are traceable, reasoned, aligned, monitored and revisable. Each decision starting from a better prior than the last.

That is not a faster system of record. It is collective intelligence. Humans stop being the middleware and go back to being the judgment.

That is what we are building at Reflective Labs. The full argument is in the essay.
